# 📖 Children's Bedtime Story — Craft Guide

> This is a **reference document** used by `/story-writing` and `/story-review`. It defines the SOTA techniques that separate bestselling children's books from AI-generated slop.

---

## 1. The Read-Aloud Test (The Only Test That Matters)

**Rule:** Every sentence must sound beautiful when read aloud by a tired parent at 8pm for the 47th time.

### How to Check
Read every sentence out loud. If you stumble, rewrite. If it's boring to say, rewrite. If a word feels clunky in the mouth, swap it.

### The Tired Parent Standard
- Is this bearable on reading #50?
- Does this make the parent smile at least once?
- Does the ending make the parent feel calm, not just the child?

## 2. Rhythm Patterns (The Secret Weapon)

Children's prose has **musicality**. Not rhyming (optional) — but rhythmic stress patterns.

### Pattern A: Trochaic (STRONG-weak) — Most Common in Bedtime Books
```
STEP step STEP step THROUGH the WOOD
LIT-tle FOX went LOOK-ing for HOME
```
This creates a heartbeat-like lull. Perfect for winding down.

### Pattern B: Page-Turn Anticipation (Short→Long→STOP)
```
She looked left.
She looked right.  
She looked up at the big, wide, whispery sky — 

                                          [PAGE TURN]

And there, sitting on a cloud no bigger than a pillow... was Moon.
```

### Pattern C: The Rule of Three + Surprise
```
She tried the tall door. Too tall.
She tried the round door. Too round.
She tried the tiny door...

It opened.
```

### Pattern D: Cumulative Build (Like "This Is the House That Jack Built")
```
This is the bear who couldn't sleep.
This is the owl who sang to the bear who couldn't sleep.
This is the moon who shone on the owl who sang to the bear who couldn't sleep.
```

### Pattern E: Wind-Down Descent (For the Last 3 Spreads)
```
The forest grew quiet.              ← 4 words
Stars blinked.                      ← 2 words
Sleep.                              ← 1 word
```
Progressively shorter sentences. Progressively softer vocabulary. The story lowers itself into sleep.

## 3. Sound Design (Words Must Be Delicious in the Mouth)

### Mandatory Sound Techniques

| Technique | Example | Minimum Per Story |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| **Onomatopoeia** | swish, crunch, plop, whoosh, hush | 5 |
| **Alliteration** | "sleepy stars sparkled softly" | 3 phrases |
| **Mouth-feel words** | cozy, snuggle, whisper, tumble, wobble | 8 |
| **Rhythmic repetition** | "step, step, step" — "drip, drop, drip" | 2 sequences |
| **Invented words** | "the whispery-wet woods" — "snugglebugs" | 1 (optional, delightful) |

### Words That Sound Like Bedtime (Use These)
```
whisper, hush, gentle, soft, cozy, warm, sleepy, snuggle,
drift, float, glow, shimmer, murmur, nestle, lullaby,
tiptoe, moonlight, starshine, dewdrop, feather, pillow,
yawn, blink, dream, curl, rest, still, quiet, peace
```

### Words That Sound Like Morning (Avoid Near the Ending)
```
jump, shout, run, crash, bang, bright, loud, fast,
race, spin, zoom, blast, roar, scream, slam
```

## 4. The Refrain (The "Again! Again!" Hook)

**Every story MUST have at least one repeating phrase that:**
- Appears 3-5 times throughout the story
- Is fun to say out loud
- A child can predict and "read along"

### Refrain Examples from Bestsellers

| Book | Refrain |
|------|---------|
| Goodnight Moon | "Goodnight ___" (pattern repetition) |
| We're Going on a Bear Hunt | "We can't go over it, we can't go under it..." |
| Brown Bear Brown Bear | "Brown bear, brown bear, what do you see?" |
| Llama Llama Red Pajama | "Llama llama red pajama, waiting waiting for his mama" |

### How to Create a Refrain
1. Identify the story's emotional core
2. Express it as a rhythmic phrase (4-8 syllables ideal)
3. Place it at: beginning, 3 beats through middle, climax moment, and very end
4. Vary the context — same words, different emotional payload each time

### Example:
```
Beginning:    "The moon was listening."        (establishing)
Middle 1:     "And the moon was listening."     (comfort during struggle)
Middle 2:     "Still, the moon was listening."  (reassurance)
Climax:       "The moon had always been listening."  (revelation)
End:          "And the moon... was listening."  (whispered, winding down)
```

## 5. Show, Don't Tell (For Emotions)

### ❌ AI-Style (Tell)
> Little Fox felt scared.
> She was happy to see her friend.
> The moral is: asking for help is okay.

### ✅ Bestseller-Style (Show)
> Little Fox's tail curled tight around her legs.
> Her whiskers did a little dance when Owl landed on the branch.
> "I couldn't find the way," Fox whispered. Owl smiled. "That's why I'm here."

### Conversion Rules

| Don't Write | Write Instead |
|-------------|---------------|
| "she felt scared" | physical sensation: "her tummy felt like cold rocks" |
| "he was happy" | observable behavior: "he bounced on his paws" |
| "she was sad" | sensory detail: "something prickly sat behind her eyes" |
| "the moral is..." | NEVER state the moral. Let the reader discover it. |
| "she learned that..." | NEVER. Show the changed behavior, not the lesson. |

## 6. Page-Turn Architecture (14-16 Spreads)

### Standard Picture Book Structure

A 32-page picture book = 14-16 usable spreads (after title, copyright, etc.)

```
Spread 1:  OPENING — establish character + world (1 sentence of action)
Spread 2:  WANT — what does character want? (show, don't tell)
Spread 3:  FIRST ATTEMPT — character tries (and it almost works)
Spread 4:  COMPLICATION — it doesn't work (end on a "but...")
Spread 5:  REACTION — character's emotional response (physical)
Spread 6:  TRY AGAIN — second attempt with help/new idea
Spread 7:  ESCALATION — bigger obstacle (rule of three, attempt #2)
Spread 8:  MIDPOINT — something shifts (perspective, alliance, setting)
Spread 9:  NEW APPROACH — fresh strategy born from midpoint shift
Spread 10: ALMOST THERE — so close! (page-turn anticipation)
Spread 11: CLIMAX — the moment everything changes (shortest text)
Spread 12: RESOLUTION — the warmth after the storm
Spread 13: WIND-DOWN — returning to safety (softer, slower)
Spread 14: GOODNIGHT — sleep arrives (the gentlest spread of all)
```

### Page-Turn Rules
- **End every left-page spread with anticipation** — a question, a "but...", a "then..."
- **Start every right-page spread with payoff** — the answer, the surprise, the relief
- **Never end a spread in the middle of an action** — end before or after, not during
- **The final 3 spreads must get progressively quieter**

## 7. Character Distinctiveness

### The "Describe in 5 Words" Test
If you can't describe your character in 5 words that a 4-year-old would remember, the character is too generic.

✅ "a tiny dragon who can't fly" — instantly memorable
✅ "a grumpy cloud who rains too much" — funny, visual
❌ "a brave rabbit who goes on an adventure" — generic, forgettable

### Character Checklist
- [ ] Has a specific, memorable quirk (not just "brave" or "kind")
- [ ] Has a visual hook (something an illustrator can make iconic)
- [ ] Has a voice (the way they speak is distinct)
- [ ] Has a contradiction (brave but scared of butterflies)
- [ ] Has a catchphrase or speech pattern (optional but powerful)

## 8. Age-Specific Humor

### What Makes Ages 2-4 Laugh
- Silly sounds ("BLOOP!" "SPLAT!" "PBBBBBT!")
- Absurdity (a fish wearing a hat)
- Repetition with variation (the pants get bigger each time)
- "I see it but you don't" (child knows something character doesn't)

### What Makes Ages 4-6 Laugh
- Wordplay (simple puns: "Are you a BEAR or are you BARE?")
- Dramatic irony (we know the surprise is coming)
- Breaking expectations (the tiny mouse is the bravest)
- Physical comedy described with sound words

### What Makes Parents Laugh (Layer This In)
- Subtle humor above the children's heads
- Self-aware narration ("Fox, who was DEFINITELY not lost...")
- Relatable parenting moments woven in
- Wit in the word choices, not in the plot

## 9. The Bedtime Contract

**Every bedtime story makes an implicit promise: "By the end, you will feel safe and sleepy."**

### Non-Negotiable Bedtime Rules
1. **Final 200 words must be physically calming** — slow words, long vowels, soft consonants
2. **The last sentence must work as a lullaby** — say it in a whisper and it should sound perfect
3. **No unresolved tension** — every worry must have a home by the end
4. **Warmth, not excitement** — the climax delivers comfort, not adrenaline
5. **A parent's arms or equivalent must appear** — physical safety cue

### The Golden Line Test
**Can you extract ONE line from the story that a parent would whisper to their child as they fall asleep?**

Examples:
- "The moon had always been listening."
- "You found your way home," Mama whispered.
- "In the whole wide sky, the best star was the one above your bed."

If you can't find a golden line, the story isn't finished yet.

---

## 10. Checklist Before Any Story Leaves Draft

- [ ] Every sentence passes the read-aloud test (no stumbles)
- [ ] Story has a refrain (appears 3+ times)
- [ ] 5+ onomatopoeia / sound words
- [ ] 3+ alliterative phrases
- [ ] 8+ mouth-feel/sensory words
- [ ] Zero "she felt [emotion]" sentences (show instead)
- [ ] Moral is never stated explicitly
- [ ] Character passes the 5-word test
- [ ] Structure maps to 14-16 spreads
- [ ] Humor layer exists (child level AND parent level)
- [ ] Final 3 spreads descend in energy
- [ ] Final sentence passes the "whisper test"
- [ ] Has one golden line
- [ ] A tired parent would smile reading this
